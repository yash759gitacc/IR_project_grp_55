Description
The Lotus Temple, located in New Delhi, India, is a Bahá'í House of Worship renowned for its distinctive lotus flower inspired architecture. Completed in 1986, it was designed by Iranian architect Fariborz Sahba, featuring 27 marble-clad "petals" arranged in clusters to form the shape of a lotus flower. The temple's serene and symmetrical design symbolizes purity, peace, and unity, inviting visitors of all faiths to meditate, pray, or simply admire its beauty. Surrounded by lush gardens and pools, the Lotus Temple serves as a tranquil oasis amidst the bustling cityscape, promoting spiritual harmony and understanding.

Culture
The Lotus Temple embodies the Bahá'í principles of unity, equality, and universal worship, welcoming people of all backgrounds to commune with the divine in a spirit of harmony and reverence. As one of the seven Bahá'í Houses of Worship worldwide, it stands as a testament to the Bahá'í faith's commitment to fostering peace and understanding among diverse cultures and religions. Visitors to the Lotus Temple experience not only its architectural magnificence but also the inclusive and egalitarian values it represents, making it a beacon of hope and inspiration in an increasingly divided world.

History
The idea for the Lotus Temple originated in the mid-20th century when the Bahá'í community sought to establish a House of Worship in India, a country known for its religious diversity and spiritual heritage. Construction of the temple began in 1980, and it was inaugurated six years later in a ceremony attended by dignitaries from around the world. Since then, the Lotus Temple has become a symbol of religious tolerance and interfaith dialogue, attracting millions of visitors annually who seek solace, reflection, and enlightenment within its serene confines. Its timeless beauty and message of unity continue to inspire generations, transcending cultural and religious boundaries.
