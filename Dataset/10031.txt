Description
Chittorgarh Fort, situated in the city of Chittorgarh in the Indian state of Rajasthan, is one of the largest forts in India and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Spread over an area of approximately 700 acres, the fort is perched atop a hill and surrounded by a 13-kilometer-long wall with seven gates and several towers. It houses numerous palaces, temples, and reservoirs, including the iconic Vijay Stambha (Tower of Victory) and Kirti Stambha (Tower of Fame). Chittorgarh Fort is steeped in history and folklore, serving as a symbol of Rajput valor, resistance, and sacrifice.

Culture
Chittorgarh Fort is deeply intertwined with Rajput culture and history, embodying the spirit of valor, honor, and sacrifice that defines the Rajput ethos. The fort served as the capital of the Mewar kingdom for centuries and witnessed several battles and sieges fought by Rajput rulers against invading forces. Its majestic palaces, intricately carved temples, and imposing towers stand as testaments to the architectural and artistic prowess of the Rajputs. Chittorgarh Fort is also associated with tales of courage and sacrifice, including the legendary Jauhar (mass self-immolation) led by Rajput women to avoid capture by enemy forces.

History
Chittorgarh Fort has a long and storied history dating back to the 7th century when it was founded by the Maurya dynasty. Over the centuries, the fort was ruled by various dynasties, including the Guhilas, Sisodias, and Mewars. It reached its zenith under the reign of rulers like Maharana Kumbha and Maharana Pratap, who expanded its boundaries and fortified its defenses. Chittorgarh Fort witnessed several sieges and battles, including the famous Siege of Chittorgarh in 1568 by Emperor Akbar, which resulted in the mass Jauhar by Rajput women led by Rani Karnavati. Despite facing numerous attacks and undergoing periods of decline, Chittorgarh Fort remains a symbol of Rajput pride and resilience, attracting visitors from around the world with its rich history and architectural splendor.
