Description
The Golden Temple, also known as Sri Harmandir Sahib, is a sacred Sikh shrine located in Amritsar, Punjab, India. Built-in the 16th century, it is one of the most revered places of worship for Sikhs worldwide. The temple's architecture is a harmonious blend of Islamic and Hindu styles, featuring marble and gold leaf adorning its exterior. Surrounded by a serene sarovar (pool of water), the Golden Temple exudes a sense of spiritual tranquility and reverence. Its central shrine, housing the Guru Granth Sahib (the Sikh holy scripture), is accessible via a causeway symbolizing the universal path to enlightenment.

Culture
The Golden Temple stands as the spiritual and cultural heart of the Sikh community, embodying the core principles of Sikhism, including equality, service, and devotion. It serves as a symbol of the Sikh faith's resilience and enduring spirit, having withstood invasions, conflicts, and political upheavals throughout history. The temple complex is open to people of all faiths, offering langar (free communal meals) to visitors regardless of their background. Through its inclusive practices and humanitarian initiatives, the Golden Temple promotes interfaith harmony and social justice, inspiring millions to embrace the values of compassion and service.

History
Construction of the Golden Temple began in 1585 under the patronage of Guru Arjan, the fifth Sikh Guru, and was completed in 1604. Over the centuries, the temple complex underwent several expansions and renovations, with significant contributions from Sikh rulers and devotees. The temple's gilded exterior, added by Maharaja Ranjit Singh in the early 19th century, earned it the moniker "Golden Temple." Despite facing challenges such as the partition of India and Operation Blue Star, the Golden Temple remains a beacon of hope and resilience, attracting pilgrims and tourists from around the world to experience its spiritual ambiance and architectural splendor.
